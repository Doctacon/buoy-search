What Is an Engineering Roadmap?

## **What Is an Engineering Roadmap?**

An engineering roadmap is a high-level visual plan that outlines the technical steps and milestones required to develop a product, feature, or system. It concentrates on the technical “how” and “when” of product development and details the engineering work needed to realize the final product vision.

Most engineering roadmaps will include information such as technical dependencies, architecture, infrastructure components, code-level tasks, and detailed timelines (often in sprints or short cycles).

Due to the nature of this content, most engineering roadmaps are targeted at technical teams (engineers, developers, technical leads) and product managers, rather than C-suite executives or other department leads.

**Understanding the Difference Between an Engineering Roadmap, Product Roadmap, and Technical Roadmap**

You might hear these terms used interchangeably. However, there are key distinctions between each and their various purposes. Understanding these differences is crucial for effective planning and communication across teams.

Let’s clarify each type:

- **Engineering Roadmap** **:** This is a detailed, visual plan outlining the technical steps, tasks, and timelines needed to build and deliver a product or feature. It focuses on the “how” of implementation, addressing specific engineering challenges like code modules, infrastructure setup, and technical dependencies. Primarily for software engineering teams, it ensures everyone is aligned on the technical execution of the product vision.
- **Technical Roadmap** **:** This roadmap takes a broader perspective, outlining the evolution of the technology stack and infrastructure over time. It focuses on strategic technology choices and platform upgrades, and their impact on the product and business goals. Used by both technical and business stakeholders, technical roadmaps make sure that technology strategy supports the overall product vision and business objectives.
- **Product Roadmap** **:** This high-level roadmap defines the “why” and “what” of the product from a business perspective. It outlines the product vision, strategy, key features, and release timelines, focusing on user needs, market trends, and competitive analysis. Used by a wide audience, including marketing, sales, and executives, a product roadmap communicates product direction and how it will achieve business goals.

To sum it up, you can think of an engineering roadmap as a more tactical guide for a specific project for the engineering team to follow, whereas a technical roadmap considers your tech stack strategy and your product roadmap details your company’s product vision for wider audiences.

When Do Engineering Teams Need a Roadmap?

## **When Do Engineering Teams Need a Roadmap?**

You might wonder if an engineering roadmap is really necessary. While each company and engineering team has its own processes and preferences, here are some key scenarios where a roadmap might come in handy:

### **When Building New Products or Features**

The most obvious use case for an engineering roadmap is when you’re building new products or features. You might consider an engineering roadmap in these instances to help with the following:

- **Provide a clear direction:** A roadmap helps define the technical vision and outlines the steps required to bring a product or feature to life. This ensures everyone on the team is aligned and working towards the same goal.
- **Facilitate strategic planning and prioritization:** Roadmaps help break down complex projects into manageable phases, prioritize tasks based on dependencies and importance, and allocate resources effectively.
- **Manage expectations:** By visualizing timelines and milestones, engineering roadmaps set clear expectations for stakeholders about when certain features will be delivered.

### **When Dealing with Complex Systems or Architectures**

One of the biggest benefits of using an engineering roadmap is that it can make an overwhelming or highly complicated project feel more manageable. Use an engineering roadmap to deconstruct your project into bitesize projects and decisions. For example, use a roadmap to:

- **Emphasize technical dependencies:** A roadmap helps visualize the relationships between different components and systems, making it easier to identify potential bottlenecks or integration challenges.
- **Guide architectural decisions:** Roadmaps provide a framework for making informed decisions about technology choices, infrastructure setup, and system design.
- **Reduce risks:** By identifying potential [technical hurdles](https://jellyfish.co/library/code-complexity/) upfront, the roadmap allows teams to proactively mitigate risks and avoid costly delays.

### **When Working in Agile Environments**

The fast-paced, iterative nature of Agile development means that sometimes it can be easy to lose the forest for the trees. Here’s how engineering roadmaps allow teams to maintain a quick, iterative development process without losing sight of overall project goals:

- **Provides context for sprints:** While agile focuses on short-term iterations, a roadmap offers a broader context, ensuring that sprint goals align with the overall technical direction.
- **Supports iterative development:** The roadmap can be adjusted based on feedback and changing requirements, allowing for flexibility while maintaining a clear vision.
- **Improves communication and collaboration:** It serves as a shared reference point for engineers, product managers, and stakeholders, fostering transparency and alignment.

### **When Scaling Products or Teams**

An engineering roadmap can serve as a north star if your team is dealing with growing pains as it scales. Here’s how:

- **Facilitates onboarding:** A roadmap helps new team members quickly understand the technical landscape, existing systems, and future plans.
- **Coordinates multiple teams:** When multiple teams work on different parts of a product, the roadmap ensures their efforts are synchronized and contribute to the overall goal.
- **Supports long-term strategic planning:** It allows for strategic thinking and resource allocation to support future growth and expansion.

How to Build Your Engineering Roadmap with 5 Key Steps

## **How to Build Your Engineering Roadmap with 5 Key Steps**

Building a robust engineering roadmap doesn’t have to be too complicated. Here’s a step-by-step guide to help you get started.

### **1\. Define Big-Picture Goals and Scope**

First, think big. You want to identify your overall product vision and goals before you start to think about the steps needed to execute the work. Document the following:

1. **Understand the product vision**. Start by identifying the overall product strategy and goals. What problems are you trying to solve? What are the key features and functionalities? This will help you understand what will make this project successful and how to prioritize work.
2. **Identify technical goals.** Next, translate product goals into specific technical objectives. This is where you start to make the process tactical. For example, what architectural changes are needed?
3. **Determine scope.** Clearly define the boundaries of your roadmap. Which engineering projects and initiatives will be included? What initiatives are _outside_ of scope? What’s the timeframe?

### **2\. Gather Inputs and Requirements**

The next step is all about information gathering. Your goal is to gather information on team [capacity](https://jellyfish.co/blog/engineering-capacity-planning/), skillsets, tools, and more to see what resources are available and where you might have gaps.

1. **Involve key stakeholders.** Consult with product managers, engineers, designers, and other relevant teams to gather diverse perspectives and insights on what resources, steps, or tasks need to be included in your plan.
2. **Conduct technical assessments.** Evaluate existing systems, infrastructure, and technologies to identify strengths, weaknesses, and potential risks.
3. **Analyze user needs.** Consider user feedback, market trends, and competitive analysis to inform your technical decisions.

### **3\. Break Down the Work**

Now it’s time to define the individual, tactical steps that your team will follow to meet your product goals. Consider the following:

1. **Identify key milestones.** Divide the project into major phases or milestones, representing significant achievements or deliverables.
2. **Define tasks and dependencies.** Next, break down each milestone into smaller, manageable, weekly, and day-to-day tasks. Don’t forget to outline any dependencies between tasks in the [software development process](https://jellyfish.co/blog/sdlc-best-practices/).
3. **Estimate timelines.** Assign [realistic timeframes](https://jellyfish.co/blog/why-target-dates-are-your-friend/) to each task and milestone, considering potential challenges and risks.

### **4\. Visualize the Roadmap**

The key characteristic of an engineering roadmap is that it’s visual and easy to understand at a glance. Spend time thinking about what visual format and information should be presented to best convey how work on the project will progress.

1. **Choose a suitable format.** Select a visual representation that best suits your needs. Popular options include Gantt charts, timelines, Kanban boards, and mind maps.
2. **Highlight key information.** Display milestones, timelines, dependencies, and assigned teams. Use color coding and visual cues to enhance clarity.
3. **Keep it concise and focused.** Avoid overwhelming the roadmap with excessive detail. Focus on high-level information and essential elements.

### **5\. Review and Iterate**

Think of your engineering roadmap as a living document. While the overarching product goal is unlikely to change, the individual tasks and steps you need to take to get there might. Use the following best practices to ensure everyone stays aligned even if things take an unexpected turn:

1. **Share with stakeholders.** Present the roadmap to relevant teams and stakeholders for feedback and alignment.
2. **Incorporate feedback**. Revise the roadmap based on feedback, ensuring it accurately reflects the project’s goals and priorities.
3. **Integrate with project management tools.** Connect your roadmap to tools like Jira, Asana, or Trello to synchronize tasks, track progress, and ensure seamless workflow between planning and execution. This helps maintain consistency and keeps everyone informed.
4. **Regularly update.** As the project progresses, update the roadmap to reflect changes in timelines, priorities, or scope.
