When it comes to software development today, efficiency isn’t just important – it’s essential. Engineering teams are continually looking for ways to streamline workflows, eliminate bottlenecks, and boost productivity. To that end, GitHub’s Copilot – an AI-powered coding assistant – has emerged as a force multiplier that helps users write and refine code more efficiently and more effectively.

But how much capacity does it actually add for engineers? We dug into data from Jellyfish’s [Copilot Dashboard](https://jellyfish.co/platform/jellyfish-copilot/) to find out.

Measuring the Impact of Copilot

## Measuring the Impact of Copilot

To assess a developer’s potential capacity, we analyzed their ability to complete discrete chunks of work – commonly represented by tickets in issue tracking systems like Jira. We define freed capacity as the difference in the average number of Jira tickets resolved per week when an engineer uses Copilot versus when they do not.

Our findings reveal that before adopting Copilot, users resolved an average of  1.95 tickets per week. After adopting Copilot, this increased to nearly 2.3 tickets per week – **a 15% boost in developer capacity**. At the company level, the total impact depends on the number of Copilot users. On average, each user resolves 0.33 more tickets per week after Copilot adoption. When scaled to the company level, this translates to an average of 48 additional tickets per month per company.

In the world of software development, a Jira ticket represents a unit of work that needs to be completed, tracked, and managed. These tickets can range from bug fixes and feature requests to technical improvements and operational tasks. Each ticket typically moves through the development workflow until the chunk of work it describes is finished and the ticket is resolved. By resolving more tickets, engineers are effectively shipping more code, fixing more issues, and accelerating the pace of product development.

Translating Capacity into Business Outcomes

## Translating Capacity into Business Outcomes

But how does this freed capacity translate into tangible business outcomes?

Engineering output is measured by deliverables – which represents **value creation** in a development cycle and answers the question: _What has been produced that advances the project or business goals?_  This can include features, components, or improvements that propel the business forward and can vary in scope and complexity from company to company.

The grouping of work into deliverables helps teams and Engineering Managers track planned work, improvements, and delivery.

Since Deliverables can vary widely between companies and teams we categorize them by the number of tickets they include as well as the type of work they might involve:

- **Small Deliverables (fewer than 24 tickets)** include tasks like bug fixes, small UI changes, single API endpoints
- **Medium Deliverables (25 – 40 tickets)** can be new features, integrations, or optimizations
- **Large Deliverables (more than 40 tickets)** like major product updates, architectural changes, or entire new modules

For the companies in our analysis, the **observed increase in capacity** would result in:

- 3 additional small deliverables per month, or
- 2 additional medium deliverables per month, or
- 1 additional large deliverable per month

In effect, a typical company leveraging Copilot could expect to free up [capacity](https://jellyfish.co/blog/engineering-capacity-planning/) equivalent to one to two additional medium-to-large-sized deliverables each month.

Factoring In Adoption Rates

## Factoring In Adoption Rates

Beyond the baseline improvements, our study also examined the role of individual Copilot adoption rates. The data clearly shows that the more frequently a developer uses Copilot, the greater the capacity boost. Power users – those who leverage Copilot more than 80% of the time – saw nearly 0.5 more resolved tickets per week compared to their counterparts with lower adoption rates who saw about 0.25 more resolved tickets per week.

How do those benefits translate downstream? If every engineer in a 50-developer company were a power user, the freed capacity would significantly increase:

- 6 additional small deliverables per month
- 3 additional medium deliverables per month
- 2 additional large deliverables per month

This nearly doubles the benefits compared to a more typical adoption profile, demonstrating the powerful impact of consistent, high-usage and the importance of [change management strategies and training](https://jellyfish.co/resources/genai-adoption-guide/).

Unlocking the Full Potential of Copilot

## Unlocking the Full Potential of Copilot

Copilot is more than just a helpful tool – it’s a game-changer for engineering teams. By increasing ticket resolution rates, it enables teams to complete more deliverables, accelerating product development and fueling business growth. As adoption rates improve, the efficiency gains compound, making Copilot an invaluable asset in modern engineering workflows.

For companies aiming to maximize these benefits, [encouraging widespread adoption and consistent usage](https://jellyfish.co/resources/genai-adoption-guide/) among engineers is key. The resulting productivity boost could be transformative, leading to significantly higher output without increasing headcount.
